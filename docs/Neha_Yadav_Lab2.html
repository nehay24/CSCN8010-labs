6. Univariate polynomial regression on the BMI feature¶

Training Polynomial Regression Models¶

  • For univariate models, we pay special attention to the BMI feature, taking it from training, validation, and test sets.

Storing Results¶

  • A list to keep the result of the evaluation for different polynomials of some degree.

Loop through Polynomial Degrees¶

  • We use the BMI data and employ polynomial functions of each degree from 0 to 5 to fit our training data.
In [58]:
bmi_train = X_train[['bmi']]
bmi_val = X_val[['bmi']]
bmi_test = X_test[['bmi']]

# Store results
results = []

# Train polynomial regression models from degree 0 to 5
for degree in range(6):
    # Create polynomial features
    poly = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degree)
    X_poly_train = poly.fit_transform(bmi_train)
    X_poly_val = poly.transform(bmi_val)
    
    # Fit the model
    model = LinearRegression()
    model.fit(X_poly_train, y_train)
    
    # Predictions
    y_train_pred = model.predict(X_poly_train)
    y_val_pred = model.predict(X_poly_val)
    
    # Evaluate the model
    r2_train = r2_score(y_train, y_train_pred)
    mae_train = mean_absolute_error(y_train, y_train_pred)
    mape_train = mean_absolute_percentage_error(y_train, y_train_pred)
    
    r2_val = r2_score(y_val, y_val_pred)
    mae_val = mean_absolute_error(y_val, y_val_pred)
    mape_val = mean_absolute_percentage_error(y_val, y_val_pred)

    # Store results
    results.append((degree, r2_train, mae_train, mape_train, r2_val, mae_val, mape_val))

14: Conclusion¶

  • Seeing on the training and the validation datasets of oil price, the adequate R², MAE, and MAPE values seen on the polynomial regression model chosen.
  • Still, the application of the model highly depends on the chosen degree of the polynomial. Higher degree models are likely to over-fit, where the model augments the noise in the training data instead of the real pattern.
  • The model also prescribes a specific functional form that captures the association between BMI and the rate of disease progression, which does not give an exhaustive representation of the rate through which the diabetes disease progresses.
  • These are generalized to include; overfitting of high-degree polynomials, and other factors in the data set which could also have an impact on disease progression may not be captured by a univariate analysis.

# Repeat the steps for multivariate polynomial and decision trees
# 1. Two polynomial models
for degree in [2, 3]:
    poly = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degree)
    X_poly_train = poly.fit_transform(X_train)
    X_poly_val = poly.transform(X_val)
    
    model = LinearRegression()
    model.fit(X_poly_train, y_train)
    y_val_pred = model.predict(X_poly_val)
    
    print(f"Multivariate Polynomial Degree {degree}: R^2 = {r2_score(y_val, y_val_pred):.2f}")

# 2. Two decision trees
for max_depth in [3, 5]:
    tree_model = DecisionTreeRegressor(max_depth=max_depth)
    tree_model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    y_val_pred = tree_model.predict(X_val)
    
    print(f"Decision Tree Max Depth {max_depth}: R^2 = {r2_score(y_val, y_val_pred):.2f}")

# 3. Two KNNs
for n_neighbors in [3, 5]:
    knn_model = KNeighborsRegressor(n_neighbors=n_neighbors)
    knn_model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    y_val_pred = knn_model.predict(X_val)
    
    print(f"KNN with n_neighbors={n_neighbors}: R^2 = {r2_score(y_val, y_val_pred):.2f}")
